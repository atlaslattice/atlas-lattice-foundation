## 1. Background: The Gap

The original `Hormuz_Escort_Briefing.md` modeled three scenarios: military escort, diplomatic corridor, and multilateral governance. None of them modeled a fourth outcome that is now happening in the real world: **Iran monetizing the chokepoint itself as a revenue-generating toll operation, denominated in a rival currency.**

This document fills that gap.

---

## 2. The Tehran Toll Booth — What Happened

On approximately March 1, 2026, Iran effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz to unescorted traffic and established a controlled corridor between Qeshm and Larak islands. Key facts:

- **IRGC naval forces** control the corridor, not the regular Iranian Navy.
- **Fee structure:** Reports indicate ~$2M per vessel transit, paid in **Chinese yuan (CNY)** via Chinese intermediary banks.
- **Volume:** ~2,000 vessels stranded or queuing as of late March 2026.
- **Oil price impact:** Brent crude reached ~$120/bbl.
- **Legislative formalization:** Iranian parliament (Majlis) is reportedly drafting legislation to formalize the toll regime.
- **International law:** This violates **UNCLOS Article 26**, which prohibits tolls on international straits "by reason only of passage." Iran's likely counter-argument: the fee is for "security escort services," not transit.

---

## 3. Why the Framework Missed This

The Escort Briefing assumed rational state actors operating within a game-theoretic framework where closure = mutual destruction. It did not model:

- **Chokepoint-as-revenue-source:** Iran treating the strait not as a bargaining chip but as a profit center.
- **Currency warfare:** Denominating fees in yuan shifts the transaction outside the dollar system, making US sanctions enforcement structurally harder.
- **IRGC autonomous action:** The IRGC operates semi-independently from Iran's civilian government. The toll may not reflect a unified national strategy — it may reflect IRGC self-funding.
- **Chinese complicity or acquiescence:** Chinese intermediary banks processing yuan payments suggests at minimum Chinese tolerance, at maximum active coordination.

## 4. The Hormuz-First Thesis

As articulated during crisis analysis: the US strategic interest in Iran has always been about Hormuz, not the nuclear program. The strait handles ~20% of global oil transit. Control of Hormuz is control of the global energy market.

The argument: if the US had treated Hormuz as a Panama Canal-style strategic asset from the beginning — securing the chokepoint directly rather than pursuing regime change or nuclear diplomacy — the current toll regime would have been structurally impossible.

Instead, decades of indirect strategy (sanctions, nuclear negotiations, proxy conflicts, Israeli-adjacent justifications for confrontation) left the chokepoint itself undefended as a standalone asset. Iran recognized this and monetized it.

The Pentagonal Council (USA, China, Saudi Arabia, Iran, India) mapping needs a new variable: **chokepoint revenue flows**. Iran's position changes from "sanctioned spoiler" to "toll collector with Chinese backing." This fundamentally alters the game theory.

## 6. US Non-Response Analysis

As of March 28, 2026, the US Navy / CENTCOM has not forcibly reopened the strait. Possible explanations:

- **Tacit agreement:** The US may be receiving indirect benefit (intelligence, oil price effects on rivals, or a classified arrangement).
- **Escalation avoidance:** Forcible reopening risks direct military conflict with Iran and potentially Chinese naval assets.
- **Strategic distraction:** The Trump administration may be prioritizing other theaters.
- **Incompetence:** CENTCOM may not have a plan for this specific scenario (toll regime vs. outright closure).

None of these are confirmed. The non-response is itself a data point that the framework should track.
