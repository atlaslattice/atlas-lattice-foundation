# Grand Accord v2.3 — Wartime Dormancy Patch
## Bilateral Accord Suspension Clause
### Atlas Lattice Foundation | March 28, 2026

**Origin:** The Real-World Validation v2 matrix identified a framework gap: bilateral accords (e.g., Saffron & Myrrh Accord between Saudi Arabia and Iran) have no mechanism for suspension during active conflict without full termination. This patch adds a "wartime dormancy" clause.

---

## 1. The Problem

The Saffron & Myrrh Accord v1.0 establishes a bilateral framework between Saudi Arabia and Iran. However, with the Hormuz crisis active (March 2026), the accord's provisions for joint maritime cooperation, trade corridors, and cultural exchange are inoperable.

Current options under v2.2:
- **Full termination:** Destroys all accumulated diplomatic capital. Rebuilding from zero post-conflict.
- **Pretend compliance:** Both parties technically in breach but ignoring it. Undermines framework credibility.

Neither is acceptable. The framework needs a third option: **structured dormancy**.

---

## 2. Proposed Clause: Section 12 — Wartime Dormancy

### §12.1 Declaration of Dormancy

Any signatory to a bilateral accord under the Grand Accord may declare the accord **DORMANT** when:

(a) An armed conflict, blockade, or military confrontation exists between the signatories; or
(b) The Pentagonal Council has declared a WARTIME RELEVANCE status affecting the signatories; or
(c) The Court of Last Resort determines that compliance is physically impossible due to conflict conditions.

Declaration requires formal notice to the Court of Last Resort and to the other signatory. The declaration is recorded on the Optical Ledger.

### §12.2 Effects of Dormancy

During dormancy:

| Provision | Status |
|-----------|--------|
| Trade corridor commitments | SUSPENDED — no breach for non-compliance |
| Cultural exchange programs | SUSPENDED |
| Joint infrastructure projects | FROZEN — no new investment; existing assets protected |
| Diplomatic communication channel | MAINTAINED — minimum viable contact preserved |
| Intelligence sharing | SUSPENDED |
| Financial obligations | FROZEN — accrued but not payable until reactivation |
| Optical Ledger logging | CONTINUES — all events during dormancy are recorded |
| Court of Last Resort jurisdiction | CONTINUES — disputes during dormancy are queued for post-reactivation |

**Key principle:** Dormancy suspends performance obligations but does NOT destroy the accord. All accumulated diplomatic capital, institutional memory, and governance structures are preserved.

### §12.3 Protected Assets During Dormancy

The following are protected even during dormancy and may not be seized, destroyed, or repurposed:

- Joint infrastructure physically located in neutral or third-party territory
- GRII nodes operating under joint governance (placed in "autonomous mode" — continue operating but governance decisions deferred)
- Optical Ledger records (immutable — neither party may tamper)
- Court of Last Resort precedents and pending cases
- Cultural adapter configurations (Asha/Khalifa — preserved for reactivation)

### §12.4 Reactivation

Reactivation requires:

1. **Cessation of triggering condition** (conflict ended, blockade lifted, or Court determination that compliance is again feasible).
2. **Mutual consent** or **Court of Last Resort determination** that conditions for reactivation are met.
3. **90-day transition period** during which obligations resume gradually:
   - Days 1-30: Diplomatic communication fully restored
   - Days 31-60: Financial obligations resume (frozen amounts payable over 12 months)
   - Days 61-90: Trade corridors, cultural exchange, joint projects resume

4. **Reactivation attestation** recorded on Optical Ledger.

### §12.5 Maximum Dormancy Period

An accord may remain dormant for a maximum of **5 years**. If not reactivated within 5 years, either party may petition the Court of Last Resort for:
- (a) Extension (if conflict is ongoing and reactivation remains infeasible)
- (b) Renegotiation (new terms reflecting changed conditions)
- (c) Termination with preservation of assets (infrastructure and precedents survive even if accord ends)

## 3. Application to Current Accords

### Saffron & Myrrh Accord v1.0 (Saudi Arabia — Iran)

**Current status:** STRESS-TESTED / DORMANT

With this patch, the status changes to: **DORMANT (§12.1(a) — Hormuz blockade)**

- Trade corridor commitments: SUSPENDED
- Diplomatic channel: MAINTAINED (back-channel via Oman, per existing practice)
- GRII nodes in neutral territory: AUTONOMOUS MODE
- Financial obligations: FROZEN
- Reactivation trigger: Hormuz reopening or mutual consent

This preserves the accord's institutional value while acknowledging the reality that Saudi Arabia and Iran are on opposite sides of an active crisis.

## 4. Chokepoint Neutrality Addendum

Per the Hormuz Chokepoint Monetization Module's recommendation (§7.5), this patch also adds:

### §13 — Chokepoint Neutrality Obligation

All signatories to the Grand Accord agree that:

(a) No signatory shall unilaterally impose tolls, fees, or passage restrictions on international straits or waterways as defined by UNCLOS.
(b) Any chokepoint monetization by a signatory constitutes grounds for suspension of that signatory's benefits under the Grand Accord.
(c) Revenue derived from chokepoint monetization during the period of violation shall be placed in escrow with the Court of Last Resort pending resolution.
(d) This clause does not apply to legitimate, internationally recognized canal fees (e.g., Suez Canal transit fees, Panama Canal tolls) that predate the Grand Accord.

**Immediate application:** Iran's Hormuz toll regime (yuan-denominated, IRGC-operated) would trigger §13(b) upon Grand Accord ratification, suspending Iran's benefits until the toll regime is dismantled or brought into UNCLOS compliance.
